Abstract

Lossy compression is widely used for video compression, but it often introduces compression artifacts that degrade the visual quality of compressed videos. Consequently, numerous deep learning-based methods have been developed to post-process compressed videos. However, previous post-processing models often encounter difficulties when there is a domain gap between the training and test datasets. Test-time optimization (TTO), a technique that finetunes the model during the test stage, has been considered an effective solution to address the domain gap problem. In this Letter, we introduce a novel TTO method specialized for compression artifacts reduction. Specifically, we propose using image pairs available on the decoder-side, the images before and after the adaptive loop filtering of the versatile video coding standard, as input and target of TTO such that the post-processing model can be adapted to the characteristics of test data. Experimental results on several baseline models and test datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in post-processing compressed videos.

Experimental Results
Performance Evaluation in BD-rate (%) under RA Configuration for the JVET-CTC test sequences. The Bjøntegaard Delta rate (BD-rate) is a metric that evaluates the compression efficiency of video codecs by comparing their bitrate differences at the same quality level (e.g. PSNR).

Supplementary Material
Comparison of TTO results obtained using different training datasets: Graphs of the PSNRs between the uncompressed (original) frames and the decoded frames after ALF with added Gaussian noise for the JVET-CTC test sequences, where we obtain the noise standard deviation that results in a similar average PSNR of the frames before ALF at 5 QPs. And result graphs of BD-rates for the experimental results where Gaussian noise-added frames (Ours, Gaussian) and the frames before ALF (Ours, ALF) are used as input for TTO, respectively. The noise standard deviations are chosen to match the average PSNRs of the frames before ALF for all five tested QPs. Both TTO results show better performance than M1, and using the input and output image pairs of ALF for TTO yields a higher BD-rate gain.
Statistical metrics, such as Cosine Similarity and Pearson correlation, were employed to substantiate the consistency of our findings across most images. We focused on three candidate difference signals: the difference before and after DBF, the difference before and after SAO, and the difference before and after ALF. To account for significant variations in pixel ranges, we assessed structural similarity following Min-Max Normalization. These variations in pixel ranges have been meticulously documented through histograms.
